Definition

Strictly speaking, an “illuminated” manuscript contains gold or silver, which reflects the light. A manuscript richly decorated in color only, without actually having gold or silver is, technically, not illuminated.

Gold leaf is not especially easy to use either. It is a property of gold that, unlike many other metals, it can be hammered thinner and thinner without ever crumbling away. A piece of gold leaf is infinitely thinner than the thinnest paper. It is virtually without thickness and has almost no weight.

Application

If gold leaf is to be applied to a design in a manuscript it is put on before the paint. This is crucial for two reasons:

  • Gold will adhere to any pigment that has already been laid on the parchment or paper, ruining the design.
  • The act of burnishing gold leaf is vigorous, and runs the risk of smudging any painted areas already around it.

One method to apply gold leaf is to brush down an adhesive medium that works with gold leaf, such as glair or gum, then apply the gold leaf on top. Once it is dry, it can be left dull or burnished to a bright shine.

A second method is to apply a sticky gesso which builds on top of the page. The gold is then applied, allowed to dry, and can be polished, giving it a three-dimensional look. The effect of the thick, curved edges allows light to catch from many angles at once. In some cases, the added gesso enabled the gilded surface to be tooled into intricate designs.
